Data Presentation Process and Results

1. How was my data collected?

I went to data.gov and found a dataset titled, "Death rates for suicide, by sex, race, Hispanic origin, and age: United States". I found the .csv that corresponded to this dataset and downloaded that along with a pdf of the data organized by its different series.

 

2. What was my question?

I tried to find a correlation between death rates by suicide per 100,000 and the year from 1950 - 2018. I eliminated factors of sex, race, Hispanic origin, or age, and instead just focused on how suicide rates have changed since 1950.

 

3. How did I create my graph?

I isolated the variables that were important to my question and created a graph using the year column as my x-axis and the estimate death by suicide rate per 100,000 in the U.S. as my y-axis.

 

4. What did I find?

Right off the bat, we have an absolute maximum for death rates in 1950 at a staggering 239.4 deaths (per 100,000). There’s an immediate drop down to 220.6 deaths in 1960 (the next available datapoint), trending downwards towards a local minimum in 1981 at 204.2 deaths.

Next, death rates rose slowly until they reached a local maximum in 1986 at 224.7 deaths. This is followed by the longest year-to-year within my range until our absolute minimum in 2000 at 177 deaths. Conversely, the trend picks up and we’re faced with our longest increase of deaths year-to-year until our last data point in 2018 at 231.7 deaths, which is the second-highest death rate since 1950, only lagging behind by only 7.3 deaths.

In conclusion, our highest deaths by suicide per 100,000 in the U.S. is at its worst in 1950, it slowly decreases until our lowest year in 2000, but ever since then, we have a steady increase that threatens to set records for deaths by suicide rates recorded.
